Death in Egypt

Burial

In ancient Egypt, burial practices were deeply rooted in the belief that life continued after death in a realm mirroring one’s earthly existence. The deceased needed their physical body preserved and intact so that their spiritual essence—comprising components such as the ka (life-force) and ba (personality)—could recognize it and thus secure access to the afterlife. Because of this belief, elaborate mummification and the inclusion of grave goods—such as food, furniture, amulets, and everyday items—were standard, especially among the wealthy.

A pivotal part of the funeral process was the “Opening of the Mouth” ritual, during which priests used ceremonial tools to touch the mummy’s mouth, eyes, and limbs in order to symbolically restore the senses and functions needed in the afterlife. Depending on one’s social status, the burial might be a simple grave with minimal goods—or, for a pharaoh or high official, a grand tomb richly furnished and sealed for eternity.

Tombs themselves evolved over millennia—from simple oval graves in the Predynastic period to elaborate underground chambers and pyramids for rulers—and always maintained the purpose of protecting the body and preserving the soul’s journey. The offerings and rituals performed were not just for mourning: they were for enabling the deceased’s successful transition into a blissful afterlife, often depicted as the “Field of Reeds,” an ideal version of Egypt where the deceased could live on eternally.


Afterlife

The ancient Egyptians saw death not as the end, but rather as a crucial transition into a different form of existence. They believed that a person’s identity was composed of multiple elements—most importantly the ka (life-force) and ba (personality), which after death could travel into the underworld realm known as the Duat. To reach a blessed afterlife, one needed not only the proper rituals (such as mummification and tomb-provisions) but also a moral life guided by the principle of Maat (truth, balance, order).

Upon death, the deceased embarked on a perilous journey through the Duat, encountering trials and obstacles. One of the most famous scenes is the “weighing of the heart,” where the heart of the deceased was weighed against the feather of Maat; if the heart was heavier with wrongdoing, the soul faced obliteration rather than eternal life. If successful, the soul would be transformed into an akh—a transfigured being—and enter the idyllic paradise of the Field of Reeds (also called Aaru), a mirror of one’s earthly life filled with fields, water, and plenty.

Rituals played a critical role in making this journey possible. Texts such as the Book of the Dead (and earlier the Pyramid Texts and Coffin Texts) provided spells and guidance for the deceased to navigate the afterlife. Meanwhile, the living had a duty: offering food, maintaining the tomb, and performing funerary rites to ensure the ka would be sustained and the ba would be free to travel.

Grave Robbers

Ancient Egypt experienced persistent tomb raiding from the earliest dynasties through the New Kingdom. Wealth buried with the dead — jewelry, funerary goods, food, and even entire sarcophagi — made tombs an obvious target for thieves; robbers ranged from local opportunists to skilled craftsmen and insiders who knew weak spots in rock and seals. Archaeological and textual evidence (including court records and restored seals) show that robbery was common enough that authorities sometimes investigated and punished thieves, while builders and priests used architectural sealing, reburials, and magical texts to deter theft.

Famous archaeological discoveries highlight this pattern: most royal burials were plundered in antiquity, which is why Tutankhamun’s roughly intact tomb was such an extraordinary find in 1922 — it had been protected by debris and later re-sealed, unlike many other royal tombs. Modern excavations continue to turn up tombs that were robbed in antiquity (sometimes leaving clues such as breaches, missing goods, or reused objects), and in several recent high-profile finds archaeologists explicitly report chambers that were found empty or disturbed by ancient robbers.

In the present day, looting and illicit antiquities trafficking remain serious problems for Egypt’s archaeological heritage. Political instability (notably after 2011) and economic pressures have driven spikes in “subsistence” digging and organised smuggling, with satellite imagery and field reports documenting thousands of looted pits at sites; trafficked items move through networks to national and international black markets. International efforts (from UNESCO, Egyptian authorities, and foreign law enforcement) have had some successes recovering objects and prosecuting smugglers, but demand in the global antiquities market and porous borders keep the problem alive.
