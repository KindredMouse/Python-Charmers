Popular Myths

The Death of Osiris

Osiris, a wise and kind god, ruled Egypt and taught his people how to live peacefully. His jealous brother Seth plotted to kill him by tricking him into lying in a beautiful chest made to fit him perfectly. Seth sealed the chest and threw it into the Nile River. Osiris's wife, Isis, found his body, but Seth also discovered it, cut it into fourteen pieces, and scattered them across Egypt. With the help of Nephthys, Thoth, and Anubis, Isis gathered the pieces, reassembled Osiris, and used magic to bring him back to life.

Osiris then became king of the Afterlife, while Isis later gave birth to their son, Horus. When grown, Horus fought Seth to avenge his father and lost an eye that was magically restored, becoming the symbol known as the Eye of Horus. In the end, Horus defeated Seth, reclaimed the throne, and restored peace and order to Egypt. - reference "The Albany Institute of History and Art".

The Battles of Horus and Set

After Osiris was slain by his jealous brother Set, Horus, the son of Osiris and Isis, vowed to avenge his father and reclaim the throne of Egypt. The gods gathered to judge between them, and Ra himself presided over the court. For eighty years, Horus and Set clashed in contests of strength, wit, and endurance. They turned into hippopotamuses and battled beneath the Nile, each trying to drown the other. Set once tore out Horus's eyes, but Isis restored them with her magic. In return, Horus captured Set and brought him bound before the divine court, though Isis showed mercy and released him, enraging her son.

Their struggle continued with trickery and humiliation—Set tried to prove dominance through deception, but Horus exposed his schemes before the gods. Tired of the endless quarrel, the gods summoned Osiris from the underworld to give judgment. Osiris declared that his son was the rightful ruler and that justice demanded his coronation. Set was defeated and sent to dwell in the deserts, ruling over storms and chaos, while Horus took his father's throne and brought peace to Egypt. reference - "thegreatcultures.com"

Ra's Journey

Each night, as the sun set over Egypt, the sun god Ra begins his perilous journey through the Duat, the Egyptian underworld. He sailes in his solar barque, carrying the light of the sun through the realm of darkness. Alongside him are gods like Set, who defends the boat, and Thoth, who records events. As Ra travels, he passed through twelve gates, each representing an hour of the night and guarded by fearsome spirits. In these shadowy realms, the souls of the dead await judgment, and the gods of the afterlife perform sacred rituals to ensure balance.

The greatest danger comes from Apophis (Apep), a giant serpent of chaos who tries to swallow the sun and plunge the world into eternal night. Every night, Ra and his companions battle Apophis in a fierce struggle between order (ma'at) and chaos (isfet). When Ra triumphs, he is reborn as the morning sun, emerging once again in the east to bring light and life to the world. His journey is symbolic for the cycle of death, rebirth, and renewal. reference - "worldhistoryedu.com"

Major Gods

Ra

Ra was the sun god who brought light and life to the world. He traveled across the sky each day and through the underworld at night to be reborn each morning. Seen as the creator and ruler of all gods, often depicted with the head of a falcon and a sun disk above it, Ra represented creation and symbolized power, life, and renewal.

Anubis

Anubis was the god of mummification and the afterlife. Depicted with a jackal's head, he protected the dead and guided souls through the underworld. Anubis also oversaw the weighing of the heart ceremony to judge a person’s soul.

Osiris

Osiris was the god of the afterlife, resurrection, and fertility. He was believed to be the first ruler of Egypt and was loved for bringing peace and civilization. After being killed and dismembered by his brother Set, Osiris was brought back to life by his wife, Isis. He then became ruler of the underworld, where he judged the souls of the dead and offered eternal life to the righteous.

Horus

Horus was the god of the sky, kingship, and protection. He was often depicted as a falcon or a man with a falcon's head. The son of Osiris and Isis, Horus fought his uncle Set to avenge his father's death and reclaim the throne of Egypt. His victory symbolized the triumph of order over chaos, and pharaohs were believed to be the living embodiment of Horus on Earth.

Geb

God of the Earth, often shown lying beneath the sky goddess Nut. His laughter was said to cause earthquakes, and he was believed to make the land fertile and rich. He was also considered the father of snakes and other creatures of the land, and his close connection with fertility made him essential to agricultural cycles.

Hathor

Goddess of love, beauty, music, and joy. She welcomed souls into the afterlife and brought happiness to the living, often depicted with cow horns and a sun disk. Hathor was also a protective mother figure to pharaohs, offering divine guidance and nurturing energy, and her temples often served as centers of music, dance, and celebration.

Nut

Sky goddess who arched over the Earth, covered in stars. She swallowed the sun each night and gave birth to it each morning, symbolizing renewal and protection. Nut was also the mother of many important gods, including Osiris, Isis, Seth, and Nephthys, linking her directly to both creation and the cycles of life and death.

Sekhmet

Goddess of war, fire, and healing. She defended the gods in battle but could also bring plagues or restore health when appeased. Her dual nature as destroyer and healer made her central to rituals meant to ward off disease and ensure balance, and her fierce loyalty to Ra exemplified the protective power of divine wrath.

Set

God of chaos, storms, and the desert. Though known for his violent and unpredictable nature, he also protected the sun god Ra during his nightly journey through the underworld. Set's complexity went beyond mere destruction; he embodied necessary change and defended order against the forces of Apep, the serpent of chaos.

Thoth

God of wisdom, writing, and knowledge. Often shown with the head of an ibis, he served as the divine scribe, keeping balance and recording the judgments of the gods. Thoth was also credited with inventing writing, mathematics, and astronomy, making him a key mediator in maintaining cosmic order and guiding humans in learning and ritual practice.

Minor Gods

Bastet

Bastet was the goddess of home, fertility, and protection. She was often depicted as a lioness or a woman with the head of a cat. Originally a fierce warrior goddess, Bastet later became known as a gentle protector of families and a guardian of women and children. Cats were sacred to her, symbolizing grace and safety within the home.

Hapi

Hapi was the god of the Nile River and its annual flooding. He was seen as a symbol of fertility and abundance, bringing water and rich soil that allowed crops to grow. Often depicted as a large man with blue or green skin and a peaceful expression, Hapi represented nourishment and balance, ensuring the prosperity of Egypt through the life-giving floods of the Nile.

Isis

Isis was the goddess of magic, healing, and motherhood. She was the wife of Osiris and the mother of Horus. Known for her wisdom and powerful magic, Isis used her abilities to restore Osiris to life after he was killed by Set. She was deeply loved by the Egyptian people and honored as a protector of families, women, and children, symbolizing love, devotion, and the power of faith.

Khonsu

Khonsu was the god of the moon and time. He was believed to control the movement of the moon across the sky and influence the passage of time and the growth of life. Often shown as a young man with a sidelock of youth and a lunar disk on his head, Khonsu was also known as a healer and protector. His light was thought to guide travelers safely through the night

Nephthys

Nephthys was the goddess of protection, mourning, and the night. She was the sister of Isis and Osiris and the wife of Set. Often associated with funerary rites, Nephthys helped guide and protect the souls of the dead. She symbolized loyalty, care, and the hidden powers of the night, supporting her family and safeguarding the balance between life and death.

Sobek

Sobek was the god of the Nile, water, and fertility, often depicted as a crocodile or a man with a crocodile head. He was associated with the strength and power of the river, which provided life and sustenance to Egypt. Sobek was also seen as a protective deity, guarding people from danger and ensuring the fertility of the land and its people.
